UX Approach

JP Carrascal

I focus on shaping product design through research-driven insights and close collaboration with design, product, and engineering teams. My hands-on industry expertise is primarily in UX research, complemented by design experience from academic and personal projects. Much of my work has focused on complex technical domains such as cloud and AI developer platforms, where understanding workflows, mental models, and organizational constraints is essential to improving the user experience.

Most of my work involves confidential information, so I can't share detailed case studies for the majority of projects. Below is one example, followed by an overview of how I typically work.

Case study: Redesigning the .NET Framework Website

The problem

The brief was simple enough: the dotnet.microsoft.com website looked outdated and wasn't connecting with younger developers. But the underlying situation was messier. The .NET framework had received public criticism (even reaching the CEO's desk) for how hard it was for newcomers to get started and the website reflected those problems directly: fragmented getting-started guides, inconsistent terminology, and a navigation structure that left developers unsure whether they were looking at current documentation or an abandoned corner of a corporate site.

The visual refresh was the easy part. The harder question was what the site was actually trying to say, and to whom.

Getting a room of strong opinions moving in the same direction

About 15 people were involved across PM and engineering, most of them senior, and most of them with firm views on what was wrong and what to do about it. A member of the marketing team was also part of the core project team from the start, working alongside the PM, designer, and myself throughout the process. Before any research could begin, I needed to do something with all of that.

I organised and facilitated a hybrid stakeholder workshop with roughly a dozen people in the room and three or four joining remotely. The goal was limited and specific: not to find definite solutions, but to get everyone's assumptions on the table. I asked attendees to say what they thought was broken and what changes they believed would help. Disagreements were fine, I captured conflicting views on the whiteboard without trying to resolve them in the room.

Afterward, the PM and I ran an affinity analysis on everything that had come up and distilled it into about ten to twelve testable hypotheses. We shared those back with the group and adjusted based on feedback until we had a version everyone could work from. This gave the team a shared starting point and meant they had already invested in the process before the research began.

The research

With the hypotheses as a guide, we ran several rounds of research over about two and a half months: perception interviews to understand how developers thought about .NET relative to alternatives, usability tests to find friction in the current site, surveys to check findings at scale, and card sorting to inform a cleaner information architecture.

Throughout, I made a point of explaining each step to the wider team: what we were trying to learn, why we were doing it that way, and what we would do with the results. Many of the engineers and PMs hadn't worked closely with UX research before. Keeping them informed as we went meant there was much less to justify after the fact.

The outcome

The research addressed several aspects of the site.

The visual design was well received: younger developers rated it significantly more favourably than the original in usability tests.

On the structural side, usability findings confirmed that navigation was a core problem: visitors couldn't find what they needed. This led me to propose a card sorting study, whose results drove the restructuring of the site's information architecture. The downloads section presented a different problem: it required too many decisions from users, who frequently ended up downloading the wrong packages. We reorganised that section as well. Both structural changes were validated in further usability testing before moving to implementation.

On the messaging side, we wanted the tagline to appeal to younger developers while accurately representing the framework. The marketing team member contributed heavily to brainstorming sessions alongside the PM and the rest of the team; from the resulting candidates, we used a survey to select the final one.

The redesigned site shipped within a few weeks of the research wrapping up, implemented by an external team. Telemetry indicated that it performed better on time-on-page and conversion metrics.

What could have been done differently

I should have involved more stakeholders directly in the research sessions, having them observe interviews and usability tests rather than just receiving the synthesised findings. The research made clear that beginners found .NET genuinely hard to get started with, not just hard to read about on the website. I think seeing that first-hand would have pushed the team to address the underlying product problems sooner. The site improved, but the onboarding experience took longer to follow.

How I work

The case study above is one example of this process in practice. Below is an outline of how I typically work:

Understand
problems → Turn insight into
product direction → Design &
prototype → Validate &
iterate

Understanding customers and their problems

My work is deeply rooted in cross-functional collaboration. When design, product, engineering, and research work together to frame a problem, teams are more likely to align on what should be built and why.

To understand the problem space, I use a mix of methods depending on the stage of the product: telemetry analysis, interviews, surveys, workflow walkthroughs, and jobs-to-be-done analysis. Design workshops are often useful to align stakeholders and explore solution spaces collaboratively.

Turning insight into product direction

Research and discovery are only valuable if they lead to better product decisions. After synthesizing insights, I work with design, product, and engineering partners to identify opportunity areas and define what should change in the product or what new capabilities we want to introduce. This often involves mapping insights to product capabilities, prioritizing improvements, and developing models to create alignment on the experience direction before detailed design begins.

Early design and prototyping

As potential solutions emerge, early designs and prototypes are validated with users to build confidence in the direction. Methods such as design reviews, usability testing, and heuristic evaluations help identify friction early and ensure solutions address real customer problems while aligning with product and business goals.

Product development and iteration

Once designs are implemented, the feedback loop continues. Usability studies, in-product surveys, telemetry analysis, and preview programs help evaluate whether the product is solving the intended problems.

I encourage product and design teams to observe research sessions whenever possible. Direct exposure to users builds empathy and often accelerates decision-making as teams see firsthand where workflows break down or where new opportunities emerge.
